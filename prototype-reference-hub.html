What is Proactive Risk Management?

Start Here · 5 min read

Proactive risk management starts with understanding your business objectives, then identifies where uncertainty exists around those objectives. It replaces "what can go wrong?" with "what are we uncertain about, and what does that mean for our goals?"

The key shift

Traditional

"What bad things might happen?"

Problem-focused. No business context. Reactive.

Proactive

"What are we trying to achieve, and what creates uncertainty?"

Goal-focused. Contextualised. Forward-looking.

Why this matters

When risk is reactive, the organisation spends time documenting problems after they emerge. When risk is proactive, the organisation spends time preventing problems and delivering certainty to its objectives.

The mindset to adopt

"Fix things, not identify things." The goal of risk management is treatment delivery, not register population. A risk register full of unactioned items is not a sign of good risk management — it's a sign of a broken process.

Understand goals and objectives. Define areas of uncertainty. This is the foundation — without business context, a risk is just a worry with no anchor.

Good

"Our objective is to complete the vendor migration by Q3. The uncertainty is around data continuity during cutover."

How to Assess Likelihood

Guides · 4 min read

Likelihood answers: "How often could this happen?" Use evidence and frequency data, not gut feel. The scale below uses plain-language descriptions tied to time-based frequency bands.

Key principle

A proactive assessment includes why you chose that level — evidence, precedent, or data. "Medium" with no reasoning is not useful. "Possible — we've seen this once in the last 2 years in a similar team" is actionable.

The Likelihood Scale

Click each level to see detailed guidance, examples, and common mistakes.

5

Almost Certain

More than once a quarter

When to select this

The event is happening right now, has happened multiple times recently, or has clear indicators it will recur within weeks. Example: phishing attempts targeting your team are occurring weekly.

Common mistake

Selecting "Almost Certain" for theoretical worst-case scenarios that haven't actually occurred. This level requires evidence of frequency, not severity.

4

Likely

Once a year to once a quarter

When to select this

The event has happened before in your area or similar environments, and conditions haven't changed enough to prevent recurrence. Example: contractor credential issues occur quarterly across the industry.

Common mistake

Defaulting to "Likely" as a safe middle-ground when you haven't checked for evidence. If you don't know, say so — "Not sure" is more honest than a guess.

3

Possible

About once a year

When to select this

Could happen under certain conditions, but hasn't been frequent. There is some precedent. Example: a misconfiguration that appears during annual infrastructure refreshes.

Common mistake

Using "Possible" as a catch-all for everything uncertain. If you have evidence of higher or lower frequency, use it. This level should be supported by "it happened about once a year" or similar data.

2

Unlikely

About once every 3 years

When to select this

Would require unusual circumstances. No recent precedent in your area. Example: a specific supply chain failure that last occurred 4 years ago under different market conditions.

Common mistake

Selecting "Unlikely" because it feels comfortable, when the real reason is you haven't looked for evidence. Low confidence is not the same as low likelihood.

1

Rare

Once every 10 years or less

When to select this

Exceptional, near-theoretical. No precedent in your organisation. Example: a natural disaster affecting your specific data centre region — possible but historically unprecedented.

Common mistake

Using "Rare" to downplay a risk you'd prefer not to deal with. Rare events can still have very high impact — the matrix accounts for this.

Remember

A proactive likelihood assessment always includes the evidence: "Likely — credential reuse incidents occur quarterly across the industry; we have confirmed 3 active stale accounts." This is what makes it useful for decision-makers and distinguishes it from a guess.

Glossary

Reference · Searchable, plain-language definitions

Risk

An identified area of uncertainty that may impact objectives

An identified threat, vulnerability, or concern that may impact the organisation, requiring capture, classification, and governance tracking.

This is NOT:

  • A resolved incident or closed issue
  • A control or mitigation action
  • A task or project deliverable

Risk Register

The authoritative, centralised record of all organisational risks

Currently hosted in System XYZ. This is the single source of truth for risk governance and reporting.

This is NOT:

  • An ad-hoc spreadsheet or email thread
  • A reporting dashboard

Taxonomy

The enforced schema of categories, fields, and ownership rules

The defined schema that every risk submission must conform to at the point of entry. Enforced at intake, not corrected after.

This is NOT:

  • Post-entry tagging or manual reclassification
  • A reporting filter or view configuration

Enrichment Pipeline

Automated processing that classifies, scores, and routes submissions

The processing layer between intake and the register. Classifies submitted risks, scores completeness, suggests mitigations, and routes low-confidence items for human review.

This is NOT:

  • The intake form itself
  • The risk register

Completeness Scoring

Measures whether a submission has enough information to be actionable

A mechanism that evaluates whether a submitted risk contains sufficient structured information to be actionable, and flags under-described submissions before they enter the register.

This is NOT:

  • A measure of how many risks have been submitted
  • A reporting metric on organisational risk coverage

Human Review Queue

Flagged submissions routed to GRC for sign-off

A workflow that sends flagged or low-confidence risk submissions to GRC staff for sign-off before proceeding to the register. Not the primary path — only triggered for uncertain submissions.

Non-GRC User

An employee outside the GRC function who reports or owns a risk

The primary audience for the risk intake tool. These are people who observe risks in their day-to-day work but don't operate inside the GRC system. The new model makes them "first responders" — GRC becomes the governance layer, not the data entry layer.

5 Questions Every Risk Should Answer

Start Here · 2 min read

1

What is the business objective at stake?

A risk without context is just a worry. Start with what you're trying to achieve.

2

What specifically creates uncertainty?

Name the concrete source of the risk — a process gap, a dependency, an external factor.

3

What evidence supports your assessment?

How often has this happened? What data tells you this is likely or unlikely?

4

What would change the risk level?

Define the triggers — events or conditions that would make this better or worse.

5

What needs to happen to deliver certainty?

Not "what control should we add" — what action, with a timeline, will reduce the uncertainty?

How to Write a Risk Description

Guides · 3 min read

A good risk description is specific, contextualised, and actionable. It tells a decision-maker what's happening, why it matters, and what the scope is — in 2-4 sentences.

The formula

[What] is happening or could happen, because [why/cause]. This affects [scope/who] and could result in [consequence].

Examples

Weak

"There is a risk that our systems could be hacked."

No specifics. No scope. No actionable information. A decision-maker can't do anything with this.

Strong

"Contractor VPN credentials are not revoked upon contract termination. 14 accounts from the last 12 months remain active with access to the staging environment, which mirrors production customer data."

Specific cause, quantified scope, clear consequence. Actionable.

Weak

"Risk of non-compliance."

With what? Affecting whom? What's the deadline? This creates register clutter, not insight.

Strong

"The Q3 DORA reporting deadline requires evidence of ICT third-party risk management. Our current vendor assessment process covers 60% of critical suppliers. The remaining 40% have no documented assessment."

Named regulation, quantified gap, clear timeline. A manager can scope the work immediately.

The test

Read your description and ask: "Can someone who doesn't know the context understand what's at stake and take a next step?" If yes, it's good enough. If no, add the missing specifics.
